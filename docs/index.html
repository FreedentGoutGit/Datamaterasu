Emissions and emitors

Since the industrial revolution, a few countries have been the major emitters of GHG and CO2. The United States, China, and European nations have contributed the most to these emissions.

Burning fossil fuels like coal, oil, and gas releases CO2, trapping heat in the atmosphere. This greenhouse effect causes global warming and climate change.

CO2 and other GHGs disrupt the Earth's energy balance, leading to rising temperatures, melting ice caps, and extreme weather events. The historical emissions from these countries highlight the need for global cooperation to address climate change.

The impact of climate change

By 2050, climate change will cause 14.5 million deaths and $12.5 trillion in losses. Healthcare costs will rise by $1.1 trillion.

Floods will lead to 8.5 million deaths. Droughts will cause 3.2 million deaths. Heat waves will cost $7.1 trillion in lost productivity.

Mosquito-borne diseases will spread, putting 500 million more people at risk.

Source: World Economic Forum : Insight report January 2024

Mitigation

By 2050, mitigating climate change could save millions of lives and trillions in economic losses. Renewable energy and efficient technologies can reduce emissions significantly.

Adopting green technologies can cut global CO2 emissions by 50%. Wind and solar can meet 80% of energy needs, reducing fossil fuel dependency.

Improving energy efficiency in buildings and transportation can save $2.5 trillion annually. Sustainable agriculture can decrease emissions by 30%.

Forestation efforts can absorb 2 billion tons of CO2 annually. Strong policies and international cooperation are crucial to prevent climate catastrophe.
